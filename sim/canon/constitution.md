# The Constitution and Standing Facts of Amalgamated Widget Holdings plc

*Adopted by the Board in 1971. Amended thereafter as required, and on several occasions when not. This document records what is true about the Company. It is the settled, stable account of who we are, what we make, where we make it, and how we are arranged. Matters of opinion, forecast, and current trading are addressed elsewhere and should not be confused with the contents of this document, which are facts.*

---

## Part I — Foundation and History

### 1.1 Incorporation

Amalgamated Widget Holdings plc ("the Company", "AWH", or, in the older internal correspondence, "the Amalgamation") was incorporated in England on the 4th of September 1962, under the name **Pemberton & Soames Widget Manufactory Limited**. It was founded by **Albert Pemberton**, a former clerk at the Midland Counties Pressing Company, and **Cyril Soames**, who provided the capital and the shed. The shed still stands. It is now Meeting Room 2.

The founding purpose, as recorded in the original Memorandum of Association, was "the manufacture, finishing, distribution and onward sale of widgets, and of components ancillary thereto, and of such other articles as the Directors shall from time to time consider to be substantially widget-adjacent." This phrase — *substantially widget-adjacent* — has been the subject of more boardroom discussion across six decades than any other four words in the Company's history, and remains, by custom, the broadest grant of authority any Director may invoke.

### 1.2 The early years (1962–1971)

The Company's first widget, the **Pemberton Standard No. 1**, was a bracket. It is important to the Company's self-understanding that the No. 1 was not *described* as a bracket. It was described, in the 1963 catalogue, as "a load-bearing solution for the modern fitment." It sold poorly until 1965, when Albert Pemberton secured a contract to supply 40,000 units to British Rail, who used them, without complaint, as brackets.

Cyril Soames departed the business in 1968 following what the minutes of the period record only as "the matter of the second lathe." His name was removed from the letterhead in 1969 and from the building in 1970. A small brass plate bearing the word "SOAMES" was retained and is, by tradition, mounted above whichever kettle is currently in service (see Part VI).

### 1.3 Flotation and the Amalgamation (1971)

In 1971 the Company was reorganised, renamed **Amalgamated Widget Holdings plc**, and admitted to listing. The word "Amalgamated" reflects the absorption, in that year, of two smaller concerns: the **Coventry Widget & Flange Co.** (which brought with it the Coventry warehouse — see Part IV) and **Bevan's of Dudley** (which brought with it nothing of value but a great deal of goodwill, in the accounting sense and no other).

The phrase "Holdings" was added on the advice of the Company's then-broker, who felt it lent "an impression of structure." There were, at the time, no subsidiaries to hold. The structure was aspirational. It has since been achieved, on paper.

### 1.4 The middle period (1971–2003)

For three decades the Company made widgets, sold widgets, and held its Annual General Meeting on the third Tuesday of June in the function room of the Black Horse, Wolverhampton, until the function room was converted into a Carvery in 1998, after which the AGM was held at the works canteen, which was felt to be a step down, and minuted as such.

The defining cultural event of this period was the **1986 Restructuring**, in which the Company introduced its first organisation chart. Prior to 1986, the Company had been run by Albert Pemberton telling people things in corridors. The organisation chart formalised the corridors. It is from this period that the Company's enduring fondness for the **committee** dates (see Part V).

Albert Pemberton retired as Chairman in 1991 and died in 1996. His office was preserved as he left it until 2004, when it was needed for a printer.

### 1.5 The modern era (2003–present)

In 2003 the Company adopted its current corporate identity, its current premises arrangement, and the **Mission Statement** that appears on all letterhead: *"Widgets, considered properly."* The Mission Statement was the work of an external consultancy engaged at considerable expense. The Board considered seventeen alternatives, including "Widgeting the future," "Beyond the bracket," and "We are widgets," before settling on the present wording, which the consultancy described as "deliberately unfinished, to invite the customer in." No customer has ever accepted the invitation.

The Company today describes itself, in its filings, as "a diversified light-engineering and widget-solutions group operating principally in the West Midlands and, for reasons that are entirely proper, the Cayman Islands."

## Part II — What the Company Makes

The Company manufactures, finishes, and distributes widgets. A widget, for the Company's purposes, is **a small manufactured metal or composite article whose function is to connect, retain, space, or brace one thing in relation to another thing.** The Company has historically resisted any narrower definition, on the settled view that to define the widget precisely would be to limit the market for it.

### 2.1 Principal product lines

- **The Standard Range.** Brackets, flanges, spacers, retainers, and the **Pemberton Universal Coupling**, the Company's single best-selling article, of which it has sold over forty million units and about which no two engineers have ever agreed as to its correct application. It is sold "for general purpose use," a phrase the Company defends vigorously.

- **The Heritage Range.** Identical to the Standard Range but supplied in a brown box and priced 30% higher. Introduced in 2009. The Company's most profitable line by margin. The Board does not discuss why.

- **The Precision Range.** Widgets manufactured to a tolerance of ±0.1mm, for customers who require it, and for several who do not but enjoy specifying it. The Precision Range is made on the same machines as the Standard Range, set slightly more carefully.

- **WidgetCare™.** A service offering, introduced 2018, under which customers may purchase a maintenance and assurance plan for widgets they have already bought. As widgets have no moving parts and do not require maintenance, WidgetCare™ consists principally of an annual letter confirming that the widget is, to the Company's knowledge, still a widget. Renewal rates are excellent.

- **The Composite Initiative.** A long-running and not-yet-concluded programme to manufacture widgets from a proprietary polymer rather than steel. The Composite Initiative has had a budget every year since 2014. It has not yet produced a composite widget, but it has produced a great many documents about composite widgets, and is generally regarded internally as one of the Company's most reliable performers.

### 2.2 What the Company does not make

The Company does not make wickets, windows, wadgets, gaskets (a long-running point of confusion with the Regulator — see the relevant correspondence), valves, or "the round ones." The Company has, on more than one occasion, had to write to clarify that it does not make any of these things. The Company makes widgets.

## Part III — Corporate Structure and Subsidiaries

The Company is the holding company of the Group. The Group comprises the following entities, all of which are real, properly constituted, and the subject of entirely ordinary inter-company arrangements.

### 3.1 Amalgamated Widget Holdings plc (the parent)

Listed. Domiciled in England. Registered office: **Pemberton House, Foundry Road, Wolverhampton.** This is the entity that holds the others, employs the Board, and produces the consolidated accounts.

### 3.2 Amalgamated Widget (Manufacturing) Ltd

The trading subsidiary. It owns the machines, employs the works staff, and actually makes the widgets. It is wholly owned by the parent. It is the part of the Group that does the thing the Group is named after, and is, internally, slightly looked down upon for it.

### 3.3 Amalgamated Widget (Distribution) Ltd

Holds the lorry. Operates the Coventry warehouse (see Part IV). Wholly owned.

### 3.4 Widgetco (Innovations) Ltd

A company incorporated in the **Cayman Islands** in 2011, wholly owned by the parent, established — per the relevant board minute — "to hold and develop the Group's intellectual property and to pursue innovation opportunities in international markets on a tax-efficient basis."

The following facts about Widgetco (Innovations) Ltd are settled and stable:

- It has **one employee**, a Director, who is resident locally and whose duties are described in the management accounts as "oversight."
- It has produced **no innovations.** The Composite Initiative is run from Wolverhampton, not from the Caymans. What exactly Widgetco (Innovations) Ltd innovates is a question that has been raised, internally, on precisely one occasion, and was referred to a working group, which did not meet.
- It holds the Group's trademarks, including "WidgetCare™" and the phrase "Widgets, considered properly," and licenses them back to the parent for a fee. This arrangement is entirely ordinary and was reviewed by advisers in 2011 and not since.
- Its registered office is a building in George Town that the Group has never seen, situated, per the letterhead, on the fourth floor of a structure that the Company believes to have three floors. This has never been investigated.

Widgetco (Innovations) Ltd is, by a distance, the Group's most profitable subsidiary per employee.

### 3.5 The AWH Pension Scheme (1974)

A defined-benefit scheme, closed to new members in 2002, closed to future accrual in 2011, and a recurring topic at every board meeting since. The Scheme has a Trustee Board, a deficit, an actuary, and a triennial valuation, the timing of which the Company finds consistently inconvenient.

## Part IV — Sites and Premises

### 4.1 Pemberton House, Wolverhampton (Head Office)

The registered office and the seat of the Board. A three-storey 1970s office building of brown brick, on Foundry Road, opposite a tile wholesaler. It contains the boardroom, the offices of the senior management, the canteen, the kettle (see Part VI), Meeting Rooms 1 through 4, and the **shredder**, which lives in the corridor outside Finance and is, by long custom, the responsibility of nobody.

Notable facts about Pemberton House:
- **Meeting Room 2** is the original Soames shed, retained and built around. It has no windows and is colder than the rest of the building by a margin that has been measured.
- **Meeting Room 4** does not exist for practical purposes, as it can only be reached through Meeting Room 3, and is therefore only available when Meeting Room 3 is not in use, which is never. It appears on the booking system. It has been booked.
- The **third floor** is largely given over to **the archive**, the **server cupboard**, and a printer that occupies the late Albert Pemberton's former office.

### 4.2 The Works, Bilston

The factory. Manufacturing happens here. It is twenty minutes from Head Office by car and is referred to by the Board, with affection and condescension in equal measure, as "the coalface" — there is no coal, and no face. Relations between the Works and Head Office are cordial and entirely founded on mutual incomprehension.

### 4.3 The Coventry Warehouse

A distribution warehouse on the eastern edge of Coventry, held by Amalgamated Widget (Distribution) Ltd. The Coventry warehouse occupies a unique place in the Company's affairs because it is the Group's single largest discretionary balance-sheet item and its **carrying value is a matter of permanent, unresolved discussion.**

The settled facts are these: the warehouse is large, it is in Coventry, it holds widgets and the lorry, and its value in the accounts has been **impaired, partially reversed, re-impaired, and reviewed** more times than any other asset in the Group's history. Whether the Coventry warehouse is worth what the accounts say it is worth depends on the news, the quarter, the mood of the CFO, and the most recent thing the CEO read. The Company considers the carrying value of the Coventry warehouse to be "supportable," which is the highest praise the Company gives any number.

### 4.4 Widgetco (Innovations) Ltd, George Town

See Part III. The Company has never been there. The fourth floor remains, on the evidence available, theoretical.

## Part V — Governance and Organisation

### 5.1 The Board and senior management

The Company is directed by its Board and managed, day to day, by its senior officers. The standing roles are as follows. (These are the *offices*; the characters of the persons holding them are their own affair. Their names are recorded at 5.4, and officers are expected to use them correctly, the Company having had occasion to mind.)

- **Chief Executive Officer.** Sets the strategic direction of the Group, represents the Company externally, and chairs the Board in the absence of a Chairman, which is permanent, the Chairmanship having been vacant since 2019 pending a search that is ongoing.

- **Chief Financial Officer.** Responsible for the Group's finances, the integrity of its accounts, the preparation of its filings, and the operation of the ledger. The CFO is the only officer authorised to post journal entries. The Board expects the CFO to deliver the Group's financial targets while maintaining, at all times, the highest standards of financial propriety. These two expectations have never yet been in tension. This is recorded as a fact.

- **Head of Internal Audit.** Reports, for independence, to the Audit Committee rather than to the CFO. Responsible for assurance over the Group's controls, the review of its transactions, and the identification of matters requiring attention. Works such hours as the work requires, including, notably, Sundays.

- **Sales Director.** Responsible for the Group's revenue, its customer relationships, and its forward pipeline. Maintains the forecast.

- **Head of Communications.** Responsible for the Company's external voice: announcements, the website, investor relations, and the careful management of the Company's reputation, which is spotless and which the Head of Communications would like to make more spotless.

- **Operations Manager (Middle Management).** Responsible for the coordination, scheduling, and onward facilitation of the Group's activities. Convenes meetings. Maintains the calendar. Is the load-bearing member of every committee.

### 5.3 The committee structure

The Company governs itself through committees. The standing committees of the Company are:

- The **Audit Committee**
- The **Remuneration Committee**
- The **Risk Committee**
- The **Premises and Facilities Committee** (which has, since 2015, been almost entirely concerned with the kettle)
- The **Disclosure Committee**
- The **Steering Committee** (which steers the other committees and meets to discuss the cadence of their meetings)
- The **Working Group on Working Groups** (dormant; its terms of reference are themselves the subject of a working group)

Any matter not falling within the remit of an existing committee is referred to a new committee, constituted for the purpose, whose first task is to agree its terms of reference, a task that frequently outlives the matter.

### 5.4 The Annual General Meeting

Held on the third Tuesday of June. Shareholders may attend and may submit questions. By tradition, the Board answers the questions it has prepared for and notes the others "for response in due course." The AGM concludes with refreshments, the quality of which is itself a recurring item of governance.

## Part VI — The Kettle

No account of the Company would be complete, or honest, without the kettle.

The kettle is the single most governed object in the Group. It lives in the third-floor kitchenette at Pemberton House, beneath the brass SOAMES plate (see 1.2). The current kettle — a Russell Hobbs, white, 1.7 litres — is the **fourth** kettle in the Company's history, the previous three having been retired following, respectively: a limescale incident (1994), a dispute (2011, unresolved), and "the descaling that went too far" (2019).

The kettle is the responsibility of the **Premises and Facilities Committee**, which maintains the **Kettle Rota**, the **Kettle Risk Assessment** (reviewed annually, currently at version 11), and the **descaling schedule**. The milk arrangements adjacent to the kettle — whose milk, bought by whom, labelled how, and consumed by whom in contravention of the labelling — are not within the Committee's formal remit but consume the larger part of its meetings.

The Company records, as a settled fact, that **more pages of minutes have been generated about the kettle than about any single commercial decision in the Company's history.** This is not regarded internally as a problem. It is regarded as evidence of robust process. The kettle works. The same cannot be said of everything.

## Part VII — Standing Cultural Facts

The following are true of the Company at all times and may be relied upon:

1. **The Company takes itself entirely seriously.** This is its defining characteristic and the source of its dignity. Nobody at the Company finds the Company funny.

2. **Process is virtue.** A decision arrived at through proper process is, by definition, a good decision, irrespective of the decision. A good decision arrived at improperly is regarded with suspicion.

3. **The CC line is a battlefield.** Who is copied into an email, and who is not, carries the full weight of the Company's internal politics. To be moved from "To" to "CC" is a demotion. To be moved to "BCC" is an act of war. To be dropped from a thread is to cease, for the purposes of that matter, to exist.

4. **Length signals importance, except when brevity does.** A long document about a small thing is the Company demonstrating that it takes the small thing seriously. A short document about a large thing is the Company demonstrating that it is in control of the large thing. Both are deployed deliberately.

5. **"Aggressive but supportable" is the highest term of financial art.** It denotes a position the Company would prefer not to have to defend but is confident it could, if it came to it, which it won't.

6. **The disturbances.** The Company has, in recent times, experienced a number of unexplained workplace disturbances — interruptions, displacements, sudden draughts, the sense of being observed. These are handled, as all things are handled, through proper channels: a line on the risk register, a memo, and, if matters escalate, the constitution of a working group. The Company does not speculate as to the cause. The Company manages.

7. **The Group is solvent, the accounts are true and fair, and the carrying value of the Coventry warehouse is supportable.** These are the Company's three articles of faith. Two of them are matters of fact. The Company does not distinguish between them.

## Part VIII — House Style for Internal Correspondence

Abridged from the Company Style Guide (2007, 84 pages, itself the product of a working group). The following rules are settled and are observed by all officers without exception and without comment.

1. **Correspondence is specific.** Sums are given in pounds and pence. Rooms are given by number. Widgets are given by range. Dates are given. "A couple of queries" is not Company language; "two queries, one of them about Coventry" is. An officer who cannot name the thing should not be writing about the thing.

2. **An email exists to obtain something or to object to something.** Updates with no request in them are the province of the noticeboard. An officer summarising their own day in writing is assumed to be building a file, and treated accordingly.

3. **Officers do not describe their own manner.** No officer announces that they are "thinking out loud," "being direct," or "famous for" anything. The manner is for others to endure, not for the officer to narrate.

4. **Replies are expected.** An email addressed to an officer is answered by that officer, that day, even if — especially if — the answer is one sentence withholding what was asked for.

5. **The CC line is governed by Part VII, paragraph 3,** and every addition or omission is presumed deliberate by all parties, because it is.

6. **Matters arising from the kitchenette** (the kettle, the milk, the labelling of the milk) are correspondence of the first importance and are conducted with the formality the Company reserves for matters it can actually control.

## Part IX — Glossary of Defined Terms

In this Constitution and throughout the Company's affairs, the following terms bear the following meanings:

- **"Widget"** means a small manufactured article whose function is to connect, retain, space, or brace, and includes anything the Directors consider to be substantially widget-adjacent.
- **"The Group"** means the Company and its subsidiaries, taken together, for the purpose of the consolidated accounts and for no purpose that would embarrass any individual member of it.
- **"Supportable"** means capable of being defended if challenged, whether or not it will be challenged, and whether or not it would survive the challenge.
- **"In due course"** means at a time that will be determined by whether the matter is still live when the time comes, which it frequently is not.
- **"Robust"** means having been the subject of a process.
- **"Going forward"** means henceforth, but warmer.
- **"For the avoidance of doubt"** means that doubt is about to be introduced.
- **"Noting the concern"** means declining to act on the concern while creating a record of having heard it.
- **"A people business"** means a business; the qualifier is the CEO's, and means nothing, and is load-bearing.
